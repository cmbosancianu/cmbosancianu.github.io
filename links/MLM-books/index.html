  1. A good introductory-level exposition of the principles of mixed-effects modeling can be found in Douglas A. Luke's book from Sage's Quantitative Applications in the Social Sciences series: "Multilevel Modeling" [Amazon].
  2. Douglas Bates' lme4 book from June 2010: "lme4: Mixed Effects modeling with R" (it's quite a technical text). As Prof. Bates has stopped working on lme4, and rather moved on to developing a programming language for technical computing called Julia, the book has never come out. Some reasons for the departure can be read here. It used to be that lme4 did not display significance levels for the estimates, the reasons behind which can be found here. Starting with the 1.0* versions, however, significance levels for glmer() are displayed, courtesy of the new maintained of the package, Ben Bolker.
  3. For nlme the standard text is a book from 2000, with a second edition from 2009 - Jos√© Pinheiro and Douglas Bates' Mixed-Effects Models in S and S-Plus [Amazon].
  4. Still the best manual for running multilevel models in R: Andrew Gelman and Jennifer Hill's Data Analysis using Regression and Multilevel/Hierarchical Models (home page for the book, with datasets, scripts, and errata). The second part of the book delves fairly deep into running the models in the Bayesian framework, resulting in far more flexibility in terms of model specifications. The first part is an accessible text for beginners.
  5. gllamm for Stata - a package for running multilevel and longitudinal models (the page also contains links to datasets and scripts). This comes with what is probably the standard book for running these models in Stata: Sophia Rabe-Hesketh and Anders Skrondal's "Multilevel and Longitudinal Models using Stata" (third edition came out in 2012).
  6. Good texts for running multilevel models in IBM SPSS are Ronald H. Heck and Scott L. Thomas' "An Introduction to Multilevel Modeling Techniques" (home page) and Ronald H. Heck, Scott L. Thomas and Lynn N. Tabata's "Multilevel and Longitudinal Modeling with IBM SPSS" (home page, with an updated 2013 edition). Both pages contain datasets and instructions on how to run such models in SPSS or Mplus.
  7. The "Holy Quadrinity" (no ranking implied by my ordering) when it comes to multilevel modeling books is probably still this: Joop J. Hox's "Multilevel Analysis: Techniques and Applications" (site, new edition in 2010), Harvey Goldstein's "Multilevel Statistical Models" (site, fourth edition from 2011), Tom A. B. Snijders and Roel J. Bosker's "Multilevel Analysis: An Introduction to Basic and Advanced Multilevel Modeling" (site, second edition from 2012), and Stephen W. Raudenbush and Anthony S. Bryk's "Hierarchical Linear Models: Applications and Data Analysis Methods" (second edition from 2002). A lengthy 160-page electronic edition of Harvey Goldstein's book, written in 1999, is still available on the UCLA webpage.
  8. The state of the art in terms of technical coverage is probably Marc A. Scott, Jeffrey A. Simonoff, and Brian D. Marx's 2013 edited volume The SAGE Handbook of Multilevel Modeling. Before this, it was Jan De Leeuw and Erik Meijer's 2008 edited volume Handbook of Multilevel Analysis from Springer. In the first case I'm annoyingly saying "probably" because I've only had the chance to read a sample chapter from the book, and look at their table of contents.
  9. An entire list of books has been compiled by the Centre for Multilevel Modeling at Bristol University. It's much more extensive that what I could provide here.
